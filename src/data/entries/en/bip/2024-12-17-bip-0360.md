## BIP 360: Pay to Quantum Resistant Hash (P2QRH)

**Author:** Hunter Beast
**Status:** Draft
**Type:** Standards Track
**Created:** 2024-12-17

### Abstract

BIP 360 proposes a new output type — Pay to Quantum Resistant Hash (P2QRH) — that uses post-quantum cryptographic (PQC) signature schemes to protect Bitcoin against future quantum computing threats.

### The Quantum Threat

Bitcoin's security relies on the Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) and Schnorr signatures on the secp256k1 curve. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could:

- **Derive private keys from public keys** — Breaking the discrete logarithm problem
- **Forge signatures** — Spending anyone's coins whose public key is exposed

Public keys are exposed when:
- An address is reused after spending (the most common case historically)
- P2PK outputs are used (early Bitcoin transactions, including Satoshi's coins)
- Taproot key path spends reveal the public key in the output

### Proposed Solution

P2QRH introduces SegWit version 3 outputs that commit to a hash of a post-quantum public key. The proposal supports multiple PQC algorithms:

- **FALCON** — A lattice-based scheme (NIST standardized as FN-DSA)
- **SLH-DSA** (SPHINCS+) — A hash-based stateless signature scheme
- **ML-DSA** (CRYSTALS-Dilithium) — A module lattice-based scheme

### Design Considerations

**Signature Size:** PQC signatures are significantly larger than ECDSA/Schnorr signatures:
- ECDSA: ~72 bytes
- Schnorr: 64 bytes
- FALCON-512: ~666 bytes
- SLH-DSA-128s: ~7,856 bytes
- ML-DSA-44: ~2,420 bytes

This presents a trade-off between quantum resistance and blockchain space usage.

**Hybrid Approach:** The proposal supports combining classical and post-quantum signatures during the transition period, so security degrades gracefully.

### Timeline Context

While current quantum computers (as of 2025) are far from threatening Bitcoin's cryptography, the proposal follows the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat model — adversaries could record today's transactions and attempt to break them once quantum computers become powerful enough.

### Significance

BIP 360 represents Bitcoin's first concrete proposal for quantum resistance. As Jameson Lopp and other researchers have noted, the transition to post-quantum cryptography will need to begin well before quantum computers pose an actual threat, given Bitcoin's slow upgrade cycle and the need for broad consensus. The proposal builds on years of academic research and NIST's post-quantum standardization process.
